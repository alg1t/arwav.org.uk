Arts, AI And Allotments


Despite having a lifelong passion for painting (and allotments), I have always took an interest in to the psychology of why these needs exist, not just in terms of the transitory benefits of immersing oneself completely into the creative process, but the question as to what motivates an individual, to single-mindedly focus on developing a very specific skill, often over many and frustrating years, with an activity that has no obvious peripheral benefits.

I'm aware that my own personnal motivation is largely derived from concerns with the limitations of personnel freedoms , specifically within the context of poorly built environments, not necessarily just in terms of tinbath poverty, but with a "green poverty", an aesthetic deficit, that has largely brought about by the segregation of man and nature.

A typical place, that many of us were exposed to, is represented by the preceeding image which was taken at the time I was resident. Our home, situated just to the right of the picture, was placed directly below the coal-fired power station, in what would be regarded as a typical Lancastrian town. This was where my formative experiences of the world came about, i.e confined within a provincial pocket of manufacturing, my bedroom window effectively framing the backdrop to the imagery of heavy industry. I would not consider it wholly unreasonable to regard this some-what Dantesque scene, a landscape of Marcusian man amongst the smoking stacks, as an additional candidate for Betjeman's ire . These areas, consisting of people, who through historical circumstance, dispossessed via common enclosures, were thrown to the machine for perpetuity, from the Jenny for the many, to the modern microcontroller and microcontrolling of Taylorism, a menchanics monster let loose to demean us all. For the majority, our entire lived experience has and to an extent still is, been captured within its insidious reach.

Their exists a great irony within the focal point, in that the station was owned by a company called Manweb and despite the utilitarianism, I think it would be perfectly legitimate to regard this scene as industrial terraforming vandalism, a Blakeian aberration whose effluence and visual imposition was conferred not just to the lungs, but also the senses of the poor. For those caught in it's nexus, it was nothing short of an environmental assault - the broke got the smoke. With regards to the concept of human freedom, little has changed, the viscous lattice has merely evolved to manWeb 3.0 whose goal is to optimise the technological process, not the human spirit

I was still young when an introduction to a different kind of landscape, had the unfortunate effect of instilling a sense of pastoral idealism. It came with an awareness that socio-economics largely predetermine an individuals environmental circumstance and the invariably accompanying constraints. But this exclusion can be mitigated and for myself, it was to paint and to plant.
